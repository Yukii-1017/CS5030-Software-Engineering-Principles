Visual Impairments


Introduction

Some people have visual impairment, which is often defined as legal blindness as a condition where the best corrected visual acuity is equal to or less than 20/200, or where the person's field of vision is equal to or less than 20 degrees. A visual acuity of 20/200 means that the person would have to stand 20 feet away to see what a person with 20/20 vision could see from a distance of 200 feet. Blind people can use a variety of assistive technologies to support common tasks, such as using the Internet (WebAIM, 2020).


Another category of visual disability is low vision - usually considered to be uncorrectable to near 20/20 vision. It is common among the elderly, but young people can suffer from this disability, whether due to genetics, trauma or disease.


The third category of visual impairment is colour blindness or colour-blindness, although it may be an overstatement to call colour-blindness a true impairment, since it is a condition with few real limitations. Nonetheless, it is helpful to be aware of colour-blindness when designing Web content.


Tips

The key principle of web accessibility for people with low vision is
perceivable - smaller, poorly magnified or low-contrast content may be difficult to perceive.


Low Vision


The Types of Low Vision

People with low vision have highly variable vision, but low vision is usually defined as a condition in which a person's vision is not fully corrected by glasses and interferes with daily activities such as reading and driving. Amblyopia is more common in the elderly, but low vision can occur at any age due to conditions such as macular degeneration, glaucoma, diabetic retinopathy, or cataracts.


The Toolkit to Help Low Vision People

  1. Screen Magnifiers and Zoom:

    Screen magnification or zooming is the most common technique used by people with low vision. Software or operating system settings can be used to magnify the entire screen or parts of the screen so that people with low vision can see it more clearly. Users can also use browser settings to zoom in on a web page or increase its default text size. However, certain content can be difficult to interpret when zoomed in or out. For example, graphics containing text may become blocky and pixelated when zoomed in, making the text difficult to read. Consider the two images below(WebAIM, 2020).


    Tips:
    To make text more legible when zoomed in, please use real text wherever possible rather than text in graphics. Pages should be designed so that no content or functionality is lost when zooming/zooming or increasing text size. Where possible, avoid horizontal scrolling - it is particularly difficult to read page content when users must scroll or pan to read lines of text that extend beyond the screen. Implementing responsive/adaptive design to support small screens and mobile devices will also better ensure that content adapts to the screen as it is zoomed or enlarged.


  2. High Contrast and Customized Colors:

    Web content with low contrast (i.e., the difference in brightness between text or graphics and their backgrounds) can be difficult to read, especially for people with low vision. Some colour combinations are not easy for anyone, especially for people with low vision. Colour contrast can be checked using WebAIM's Contrast Checker to ensure that it meets the WCAG 2.1 AA guidelines.


    While authors should ensure that their content has adequate contrast, certain users with poor vision may require greater contrast. Some may benefit from specific colour combinations, such as blue text on a yellow background. Users with poor vision can enable high contrast mode, or override the default colours through settings in their operating system or web browser. With these features enabled, users may not see the author-defined page colours ("Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1", 2020).


    Tips:
    Maximize the contrast of your web pages, including graphics, fonts, and backgrounds. Ensure that colour is not used as the only way to indicate or differentiate meaning or content.



Color-blindness


Types of Color-blindness

Color blindness or lack of color vision is the inability to distinguish between certain shades of color. This is an interesting topic due to its complexity. There are three types of photoreceptors in the eye. Each is responsible for detecting different wavelengths of color. In addition, there are photoreceptors that detect the intensity of light, called rods. If these types of photoreceptors are missing or they are not functioning correctly, color blindness may occur. The most common type of color blindness is a lack of red and green - this does not mean that you cannot see red and/or green, but certain shades of red and green may be difficult to distinguish.


Tips:
The key principle of web accessibility for users with color-blindness is:
Perceivable: the difference between certain color combinations may be difficult to perceive.


The Toolkit to help color-blindness people

Tips:
Make sure that colors are not the only method of conveying important information.


Designing for Color-blindness

There is no need to get rid of colour when designing web content. It is not necessary to convert all images to black and white or get rid of images altogether. In fact, it may not be necessary to make any changes at all. Most of the time, colour is irrelevant to understanding what’s on the Web. However, when colours are used to convey or differentiate information (such as pie charts or bar charts, or the use of green and red text to indicate pass/fail or good/bad, etc.), this information should be provided in other ways as well (WebAIM, 2020).

Blindness

People who are blind use the Web.

While most blind people do have some degree of vision, any vision they do have is usually not enough to view Web content. This means that computer monitors and mice (which are visual inputs) are much less useful to blind people.


The Toolkit to Help Blindness People

Screen readers:
Blind people are able to listen to Web content. A number of software programs called screen readers have been developed that convert text and other information from Web pages into synthesized speech. Such as Screen readers that 'Software that reads text on the screen with a synthetic voice and Refreshable braille displays that A device that dynamically displays lines of text on the screen in braille (Brewer, 1999). In addition to simply reading the screen, screen readers allow users to view Web content in a variety of ways.


Common screen readers include JAWS, NVDA, and JAWS (WebAIM, 2020).


Screen readers can also be used by those who are both deaf and blind using a refreshable Braille device that converts the text into Braille rather than speech.

         Figure 3. The Screen reader (WebAIM, 2020)


Devices such as this have small pins that can be raised or lowered to form Braille characters which the deaf-blind individual can feel.


Deaf and blind people can also use refreshable Braille devices to convert screen readers to Braille instead of speech. The small pins of such devices can be raised or lowered to create Braille characters that can be perceived by deaf-blind people. Although screen readers cannot always completely replace the visual experience, when accessibility is considered, they can provide screen reader users with a high degree of access to most Web content. Screen readers cannot analyse images to extract meaning. In order to understand what an image's alternative text conveys, it must be provided with content. Similarly, a sighted user could scan a web page and quickly determine how the page is organized and make visual correlations or distinctions between content based on layout, proximity, or visual design. Screen readers cannot perform this operation. However, pages that are properly structured and contain appropriate code associations can greatly enhance the ability of screen reader users to have the same experience (©WebAIM, 2020).
